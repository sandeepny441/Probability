When a servicer (or an investor buying servicing) prices MSRs, they're essentially answering one question: _what is the present value of the future servicing fee income from these loans, minus what it will cost me to service them, adjusted for how long the loans will actually last?_ Every factor below feeds that math:

The intuition behind why each category matters: the servicing fee looks tiny — a quarter point per year — but it lasts as long as the loan does, so _how long the loan survives_ dominates everything. That's why prepayment risk is the single biggest driver. 

A loan with a note rate well above the current market is a refinance waiting to happen, so its servicing might only last two or three years and the MSR is worth little. 

A low-rate loan the borrower will never refinance can pay that fee for a decade, making it far more valuable. This is also why MSR values move _opposite_ to the rest of the mortgage business: when rates rise, originations suffer but servicing portfolios become gold, which is how big servicers hedge their origination income.

Credit quality works on the cost side rather than the revenue side. A delinquent loan doesn't just stop being profitable — it becomes actively expensive, because the servicer has to fund collections, loss mitigation, and (especially on Ginnie Mae/FHA/VA pools) advance the missed principal, interest, taxes, and insurance out of its own pocket while waiting to be reimbursed. 

So a pool of 780-FICO, low-LTV conventional loans in non-judicial foreclosure states prices meaningfully richer than an FHA pool in states where a foreclosure takes three years.

The tie-back to your earlier infographics: when a lender like UWM prices a loan on the daily rate sheet, the expected MSR value (or the SRP a buyer will pay for it) is baked into the base price as _extra revenue_ the lender expects beyond the MBS sale. 

Richer servicing values mean the lender can offer a better price to the borrower or fund larger lender credits and LPC — so factors like FICO, loan size, and escrows quietly influence the borrower's rate twice: once through LLPAs, and again through what the servicing strip is worth.
