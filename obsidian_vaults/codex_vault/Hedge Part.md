Think of the hedge desk as running a running scoreboard all day: on one side, the _expected fundings_ from the pipeline (every locked loan, weighted by its pull-through probability); on the other, the _TBA shorts_ covering them. Anything that moves either side of that scoreboard forces a trade. New locks come in every few minutes and add uncovered risk, so the desk sells more TBAs, usually in round $1M lots. Loans fund and get allocated to pools, which retires both the risk and the corresponding short. Locks get withdrawn or expire, which means the desk is suddenly over-hedged and buys shorts back. And stage migrations matter too — a loan moving from application (70% weight) to clear-to-close (97% weight) adds coverage need without any new lock. Modern hedge platforms (MCT, Optimal Blue Hedging, Polly) pull the pipeline from the LOS every 15–60 minutes, mark it against live TBA quotes, and flag when net exposure drifts outside a tolerance band — commonly ±1–2% of the pipeline. Inside the band, do nothing (trading costs money); outside it, trade back to neutral.

The expensive part is that rate moves change _both_ sides at once. A rally makes pull-through drop (borrowers re-shop), so the desk must buy back TBAs — at now-higher prices. A selloff makes pull-through spike (everyone rushes to close), so the desk must sell more TBAs — at now-lower prices. Systematically buying high and selling low is the realized cost of that free borrower option, and it's a line item in the margin on the rate sheet.

Here's how a reprice day actually looks from the desk's screen:The morning sheet is published with a small cushion baked into the margin — enough to absorb normal noise, typically on the order of an eighth of a point of price. The capital markets desk then watches the current-coupon TBA against the level used at publication. Cross the lower threshold (often 10–15 ticks, roughly 0.3–0.5 in price on volatile days) and a "reprice for the worse" goes out to every LO and broker, usually within minutes. Two asymmetries are worth knowing. Lenders reprice _worse_ fast and _better_ slowly — worsening reprices protect the margin, while improving ones are discretionary and often held back unless competitors move. And the reason speed matters on the downside is adverse selection: the moment the market sells off, every LO watching an MBS feed rushes to lock at the stale price. Those panic locks land in the pipeline at above-market prices, the hedge desk has to short TBAs at the new lower levels to cover them, and the gap comes straight out of the lender's pocket. A slow reprice on a bad day is one of the most expensive operational failures in mortgage banking — which is why big wholesalers sometimes reprice three or four times in an afternoon around a hot CPI print or Fed day.

So the loop closes like this: the hedge desk's live mark on the pipeline is the same signal that trips the reprice, the reprice throttles how much mispriced volume can flow in, and whatever slippage still occurs gets measured and fed back into tomorrow's margin. The rate sheet and the hedge position are really one book viewed from two sides.
