Dodd-Frank’s loan-originator compensation rules are implemented mainly through **Regulation Z §1026.36**. The key distinction is between:

- **Individual LO**
- **Mortgage broker/company employing the LO**
- **Retail lender/creditor**

## Payment Permutations

|Structure|Allowed?|Financial relationship|
|---|---|---|
|Lender pays broker; broker pays individual LO|Yes|Broker remains independent from lender; wholesale agreement governs compensation|
|Borrower pays broker; broker pays individual LO|Yes|Lender funds/underwrites the loan but pays no LO compensation|
|Lender and borrower both pay broker/LO on same loan|**No**|Prohibited dual compensation|
|Borrower pays lender’s origination charge; lender pays its employee LO|Yes|Payment to the creditor is not treated as direct payment to the LO|
|Seller credit pays broker compensation|Consumer-paid treatment generally applies|Cannot also receive lender compensation|
|Lender pays broker and gives borrower a closing-cost credit|Yes, if credit covers costs other than additional LO compensation|Still lender-paid compensation|
|Borrower pays an individual LO directly while lender also pays that LO|**No**|Direct dual compensation|
|Borrower pays broker company; company pays its employee LO|Yes|Express exception permitting the broker to compensate its individual LO|
|Table-funded correspondent receives investor compensation|Usually treated as a loan originator, not a true creditor|Subject to broker-style compensation restrictions|
|True correspondent funds/closes as creditor, then sells loan|Generally creditor treatment|Its employee LOs may be paid by the correspondent|

The dual-compensation rule applies **loan by loan**. If the borrower directly pays any loan originator, no lender or other person may also provide transaction-specific LO compensation. [Current Regulation Z and official interpretation](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy/regulations/1026/2020-12-28/36/)

## How Flexible Is the LO?

A broker can generally offer:

- **Lender-paid compensation:** usually embedded in wholesale pricing.
- **Borrower-paid compensation:** separately charged to the borrower.
- Both models across different transactions, but **never both on the same transaction**.

Borrower-paid compensation may differ between transactions, but it cannot be calculated from another loan term. Lender-paid compensation generally follows the broker’s compensation agreement with that lender.

Neither the broker nor the individual LO may be paid more because of:

- Interest rate or APR
- Points or lender credits
- Loan product
- Prepayment penalty
- Property or collateral type
- Any proxy that predictably tracks those terms

Permissible methods include fixed dollars per loan, a fixed percentage of loan amount, volume, hours worked, pull-through, file quality, and long-term loan performance. [CFPB official interpretations](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy/regulations/1026/interp-36/)

## Relationship With the Lender

For a wholesale brokered loan, the individual LO usually does **not** become the lender’s employee or agent merely because the lender funds broker compensation:

```
Lender-paid:   Lender → Broker company → Individual LO
Borrower-paid: Borrower → Broker company → Individual LO
Retail:        Borrower → Creditor; Creditor → Employee LO
```

The broker’s lender relationship remains contractual: approval, underwriting, funding, compensation agreement, representations, indemnification, early-payoff provisions, and repurchase obligations. Changing from lender-paid to borrower-paid compensation changes the **payment source**, not necessarily that wholesale relationship.

The LO also cannot steer the borrower toward the lender or loan producing greater compensation unless the transaction is in the consumer’s interest. [Regulation Z steering interpretation](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy/regulations/1026/interp-36/)

State licensing, wage-and-hour, broker-fee, disclosure, and independent-contractor rules can impose additional restrictions. The CFPB also withdrew an older compensation bulletin in May 2025, so current compliance should be based on the regulation and official interpretations rather than that bulletin. [CFPB loan-origination resources](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/compliance/compliance-resources/mortgage-resources/loan-origination-rule/)
