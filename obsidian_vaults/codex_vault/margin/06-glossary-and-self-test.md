# 06. Glossary and Self-Test

## Glossary

### Wholesale Lender

A lender that works through independent mortgage brokers rather than relying only on its own retail loan officers.

### Mortgage Broker

The intermediary who works with the borrower, shops lenders, helps package the file, and guides the borrower through the process.

### Par

The loan's principal balance, treated as `100.00`.

On a `$400,000` loan:

```text
100.00 = $400,000
101.00 = $404,000
99.50 = $398,000
```

### Point

One percent of the loan amount.

On a `$400,000` loan:

```text
1 point = 1.00% = $4,000
```

### Basis Point

One hundredth of one percent.

```text
1 bp = 0.01%
100 bps = 1 point
```

On a `$400,000` loan:

```text
1 bp = $40
```

### Secondary Market Premium

The amount above par that the lender can receive when selling or financing the loan.

If a `$400,000` loan sells at `101.50`, the premium is:

```text
$406,000 - $400,000 = $6,000
```

### Servicing Right

The right to service the loan: collect payments, administer escrow, handle borrower service, and manage delinquency workflows.

This right can have value because servicing may generate future cash flows.

### Gain on Sale

The lender's gain from monetizing the loan, usually connected to sale price, servicing value, and related economics.

In this packet, we use a simplified economic version:

```text
loan market premium + servicing value - transaction costs
```

### Lender Credit

A credit the lender gives the borrower, often to help pay closing costs. It helps win the loan but consumes lender economics.

### Broker Compensation

Payment to the broker. In lender-paid comp, the wholesale lender pays it, and it becomes part of the lender's transaction cost.

### Pull-Through

The percentage of locked loans that actually close.

Bad pull-through can hurt hedging and pricing economics.

### Hedge Leakage

Money lost because the hedge does not perfectly offset market movement, fallout, extensions, renegotiations, or timing differences.

### Repurchase Risk

Risk that the lender may have to buy back a loan or compensate an investor because of defects, early defaults, or representation and warranty issues.

## Self-Test

### Question 1

A `$400,000` loan sells at `101.25`. What is the secondary market premium?

Answer:

```text
101.25% x $400,000 = $405,000
$405,000 - $400,000 = $5,000
```

### Question 2

Servicing is worth `50 bps` on a `$400,000` loan. What is the servicing value?

Answer:

```text
50 bps = 0.50%
0.50% x $400,000 = $2,000
```

### Question 3

Broker comp increases by `25 bps` on a `$400,000` loan. How much more does that cost the lender?

Answer:

```text
25 bps x $40 per bp = $1,000
```

### Question 4

Why can a lower-rate loan be less profitable for the lender?

Answer:

```text
The lower rate may reduce the sale premium or require the lender to give up more value to win the borrower.
If the lender also pays broker comp, borrower credits, and normal costs, the remaining margin can become thin.
```

### Question 5

Why might a lender accept a loan with little or no profit?

Answer:

```text
It may want broker loyalty, market share, platform volume, future refinance opportunities, servicing scale, or strategic product presence.
But that only works if the broader pool of loans creates enough value.
```

## Final Mental Model

When you look at a wholesale mortgage transaction, ask:

```text
What did the lender create?
What did the lender sell or keep?
What did the lender pay away?
What did the lender spend to manufacture the loan?
What risks stayed behind?
```

If you can answer those five questions, you understand the transaction.
