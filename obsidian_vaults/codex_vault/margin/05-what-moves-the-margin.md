# 05. What Moves the Margin

This file explains why wholesale lender economics move around.

## The Master Equation

The wholesale lender wants:

```text
Market value + servicing value + fees + carry
>
broker comp + borrower credits + ops cost + hedge cost + risk
```

Everything below either increases the left side or increases the right side.

## Things That Can Increase Profit

### Better Secondary Market Execution

If the lender can sell or securitize loans at better prices, it keeps more value.

This can come from:

```text
Scale
Investor relationships
Loan quality
Efficient pooling
Better hedging
Better timing
Product expertise
```

### Stronger Servicing Value

Servicing is more valuable when future servicing cash flows are expected to last longer and cost less to manage.

Servicing can be worth more when:

```text
The loan is less likely to prepay quickly.
The borrower is likely to perform.
The servicing cost is low.
The coupon is attractive.
The servicer has scale.
```

### Lower Operating Cost

A lender with efficient systems can process a loan for less.

Lower cost can come from:

```text
Automation
Clean broker submissions
Fast underwriting
Fewer conditions
Lower rework
Tighter quality control
```

### Better Pull-Through

Pull-through means locked loans actually close.

If lots of locked loans fall out, the lender's hedge can leak money. Better pull-through makes pricing and hedging cleaner.

### Clean Credit and Documentation

Clean files reduce defect risk, repurchase risk, and servicing pain.

The lender may not see that as cash on day one, but it affects expected economics.

## Things That Can Decrease Profit

### Aggressive Pricing to Win Volume

The lender may give up margin to win market share.

This can show up as:

```text
Lower borrower rate
More borrower credit
Higher broker comp support
Promotions
Special pricing
```

This can be rational if the lender wants broker loyalty, scale, or long-term volume. But it reduces per-loan economics.

### Lower Servicing Value

Servicing value can fall if the loan is expected to prepay quickly, become expensive to service, or create more risk.

For example:

```text
Fast refinance probability
Higher delinquency risk
Smaller loan balance
Higher servicing cost
Lower expected life
```

### Messy Files

A file that needs a lot of manual work can eat margin quickly.

The sale premium might be the same, but the manufacturing cost is not.

### Lock Extensions and Renegotiations

If a loan takes longer than expected, the lender may have to extend the lock.

If rates move in the borrower's favor, the lender may also face pressure to renegotiate.

Both can reduce economics.

### Defects and Repurchase Risk

A loan can look profitable on day one and become painful later if an investor finds defects or the loan defaults early.

That is why expected risk belongs in the math, even if it is not a cash expense at closing.

## Why a Lender Might Accept a Thin Loan

A thin loan can still make business sense.

Possible reasons:

```text
Preserve a broker relationship
Win future volume
Keep operational capacity utilized
Defend market share
Build servicing scale
Cross-subsidize across a pool of loans
Compete in a strategic product
```

The danger is obvious:

```text
Volume without margin is not automatically good.
```

If the lender loses a little money on each loan and cannot make it back through servicing, scale, efficiency, or future business, the strategy breaks.

## The Big Strategic Tradeoff

Wholesale lenders live between two pressures.

On one side:

```text
Offer sharper pricing to brokers and borrowers.
```

On the other side:

```text
Keep enough economics to cover cost, risk, and profit.
```

That is the whole game.

The lender is constantly deciding how much value to pass through to win the loan and how much to retain as margin.
