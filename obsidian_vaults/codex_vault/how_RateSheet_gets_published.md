A quick walkthrough of how it comes together each day: overnight and pre-market, the capital markets desk watches TBA MBS trading (the forward market where conventional loans are effectively sold) — those prices, plus Treasury yields and servicing values, set the raw "base price" for each note rate. 

The engine then layers on the risk-based adjustments (LLPAs) for credit score, LTV, occupancy, and so on, stretches pricing across lock periods (longer locks cost more because of hedge risk), and finally bakes in the lender's margin and compensation structure — which is where LPC from your earlier infographic plugs in, since broker comp is built into the price the borrower sees.

The rate sheet typically publishes mid-morning Eastern once MBS trading gives a clear read, and if the market moves more than the lender's tolerance during the day, the engine triggers a reprice and a new sheet goes out — sometimes several times on volatile days.
