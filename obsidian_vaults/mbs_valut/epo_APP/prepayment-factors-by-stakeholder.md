# Loan Prepayment (Early Payoff) Factors by Stakeholder Perspective

This document organizes the key factors that influence whether a loan is likely to be paid off early (prepaid), viewed from three critical perspectives in the mortgage ecosystem.

---

## 1. Borrower Perspective

Factors that influence a borrower's *decision and ability* to prepay their loan.

| Factor                        | Description                                                                 | Why It Matters to the Borrower                          | Typical Effect on Prepayment Likelihood |
|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Rate Incentive (Note vs Market Rate)** | Spread between the borrower's current rate and current market rates        | Direct financial savings from refinancing               | Strong positive (larger spread = higher likelihood) |
| **Credit Score (FICO)**       | Borrower's current credit profile                                           | Determines eligibility and pricing for new loans        | Higher FICO → easier to refinance → higher prepay risk |
| **Current Equity / LTV / CLTV** | Amount of equity built up in the home                                      | Enables cash-out or rate/term refinancing               | More equity → higher ability to refinance or sell |
| **Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)** | Borrower's current debt burden relative to income                          | High DTI can limit new loan approval                    | Lower DTI → easier to qualify for refinance |
| **Life Events**               | Marriage, divorce, children, job changes, retirement, inheritance           | Major triggers for moving, cash-out, or debt consolidation | Very strong (hard to quantify but powerful) |
| **Home Price Appreciation**   | Increase in property value over time                                        | Increases equity and refinancing options                | Positive effect |
| **Loan Purpose (Purchase vs Refi vs Cash-out)** | Original intent behind the loan                                            | Cash-out loans often indicate higher financial flexibility | Cash-out originations tend to prepay faster |
| **Prepayment Penalty**        | Existence and remaining duration of any prepayment penalty                 | Directly increases cost of refinancing                  | Strong negative (while penalty is active) |
| **Employment Stability**      | Job security, self-employment status, income variability                    | Affects ability to qualify for new financing            | Stable employment → higher prepay risk |
| **Borrower Age / Life Stage** | Proximity to retirement or major life transitions                           | Older borrowers may downsize or pay off mortgage        | Increases with age in many segments |
| **Liquidity / Cash Position** | Borrower's available savings and reserves                                   | Ability to pay off loan entirely or make large principal payments | Higher liquidity → higher prepayment risk |
| **Emotional / Behavioral Factors** | Frustration with servicer, desire for simplicity, "peace of mind"          | Non-financial reasons for paying off debt               | Can be significant in certain borrower segments |

---

## 2. Loan Officer / Originator Perspective

Factors that loan officers monitor because they directly affect their pipeline, revenue, and client retention.

| Factor                                | Description                                                           | Why It Matters to the Loan Officer                            | Typical Effect on Prepayment Likelihood                  |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rate Spread Opportunity**           | Current market rates vs borrower's existing rate                      | Primary trigger for refinance inquiries                       | Larger spread = higher inbound refinance volume          |
| **Client Relationship Strength**      | How well the loan officer knows and stays in touch with the borrower  | Strong relationships can lead to repeat business or referrals | Can accelerate or delay prepayment depending on behavior |
| **Credit Profile Changes**            | Improvement (or deterioration) in borrower's credit since origination | Better credit = better refinance options                      | Positive correlation with prepayment                     |
| **Equity Position**                   | Current LTV / available equity                                        | Determines what loan products the borrower can access         | More equity = more options = higher prepay risk          |
| **Life Event Awareness**              | Loan officer's knowledge of major borrower life changes               | Allows proactive outreach for refinance or new purchase       | Strong leading indicator                                 |
| **Competitive Pressure**              | Activity from other lenders targeting the same borrower               | Direct threat of losing the client to refinancing             | Increases prepayment probability                         |
| **Marketing / Lead Generation**       | Loan officer's own marketing and lead sources                         | Can generate refinance volume from existing or new clients    | Can increase prepayments in their book                   |
| **Borrower Financial Sophistication** | How financially aware and proactive the borrower is                   | Sophisticated borrowers monitor rates more closely            | Higher likelihood of timely refinancing                  |
| **Existing Pipeline Overlap**         | Whether the loan officer has other loans from the same borrower       | Prepayment of one loan may affect others                      | Complex (can be positive or negative)                    |

## 3. Lender / Servicer / Investor Perspective

Factors that matter to the institutions that fund, service, or invest in the loans (most directly tied to actual prepayment modeling).

| Factor                              | Description                                                                 | Why It Matters to Lender / Servicer / Investor         | Typical Effect on Prepayment Likelihood |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Rate Incentive (Core CPR Driver)** | Note rate vs prevailing mortgage rates                                     | Single strongest predictor of refinancing              | Very strong positive |
| **Credit Score (FICO)**             | Current and original FICO score                                            | Higher credit borrowers have more refinance options     | Higher FICO → higher prepay risk |
| **Loan-to-Value (LTV) / CLTV**      | Current combined loan-to-value ratio                                       | Low LTV enables refinancing and cash-out                | Lower LTV → higher prepay risk |
| **Loan Age (Seasoning)**            | Months since origination                                                   | Prepayment ramps up then declines over time             | Strong non-linear effect (peak ~30-40 months) |
| **Original Term**                   | 15-year vs 30-year vs other                                                | Shorter-term loans tend to prepay differently           | 15-year loans often prepay faster |
| **Loan Purpose**                    | Purchase, Rate/Term Refinance, Cash-out Refinance                          | Cash-out loans historically prepay significantly faster | Cash-out > Rate/Term Refi > Purchase |
| **Property Type**                   | Single-family, condo, 2-4 units, manufactured, etc.                        | Different property types have different prepay behavior | Varies by segment |
| **Occupancy Type**                  | Primary residence, second home, investment property                        | Investment properties behave differently                | Often higher prepayment on investment properties |
| **Documentation Type**              | Full doc, alt doc, no doc / stated income                                  | Lower documentation loans have shown different behavior | Historically higher prepay risk |
| **DTI Ratio**                       | Debt-to-income ratio at origination (and current)                          | High DTI can constrain future refinancing ability       | Lower DTI → higher prepay risk |
| **Loan Size**                       | Original and current unpaid principal balance                              | Larger loans have higher absolute dollar incentive      | Larger loans tend to prepay faster |
| **Prepayment Penalty Status**       | Whether penalty is still active and its severity                           | Direct friction against refinancing                     | Strong negative while active |
| **Second Lien / Piggyback**         | Presence of a second mortgage                                              | Increases effective CLTV and complicates refinancing    | Generally reduces prepayment speed |
| **Geographic Factors**              | State, MSA, local home price trends, unemployment                          | Significant regional variation in prepayment behavior   | Can be very strong (e.g., CA vs TX) |
| **Servicing Quality / Customer Experience** | Quality of servicing and borrower communications                          | Poor servicing can drive borrowers to refinance away    | Poor servicing increases prepayment |
| **Media / Market Sentiment**        | News coverage and general sentiment around interest rates                  | Can accelerate borrower awareness and action            | Positive during high-visibility rate drop periods |
| **Economic Environment**            | Unemployment rate, GDP growth, consumer confidence                         | Strong economy can increase both refinancing and home sales | Complex (usually positive in strong economies) |

---

## Summary Notes

- **Borrowers** are driven by **financial incentive + life circumstances + ability to act**.
- **Loan Officers** focus on **relationship triggers and competitive threats**.
- **Lenders / Investors** care most about **quantifiable, modelable factors** that drive Conditional Prepayment Rate (CPR).

Many factors appear in multiple columns but with different weighting and implications depending on the stakeholder.

## How to Read the Chart

The interactive chart in the model visualizes two related but distinct concepts over a 30-year horizon:

### Blue Line – Remaining Principal (%)
- Shows what percentage of the **original loan balance** is still outstanding at each future month.
- Declines due to two forces:
  1. **Scheduled amortization** (normal principal paydown)
  2. **Prepayments** (extra principal payments from refinancing, sale, or cash)

A steeper blue line means faster principal is leaving the loan (higher prepayment activity).

### Red Line – Cumulative Payoff Probability (%)
- Shows the **probability** that the loan has been fully prepaid by that month.
- It is **not** the same as the percentage of principal paid.
- This is a survival analysis style curve: once the loan is considered "paid off" in the model (high cumulative prepayment probability), it stays at 100%.

### Key Ways to Interpret the Chart

- **Fast prepayment scenario**: Red line rises quickly toward 100%. Blue line drops sharply at the same time.
- **Slow prepayment scenario**: Red line stays low for a long time. Blue line declines slowly (mostly from scheduled payments).
- **Crossing behavior**: When the red line is steep, the blue line usually bends downward noticeably — this is the period of highest refinancing or sale activity.
- **Weighted Average Life (WAL)**: Roughly the point where the blue line has come down significantly (the "center of gravity" of principal payments).
- **5-Year Payoff Probability**: Look at the height of the red line at month 60. This is one of the headline metrics shown above the chart.

The chart is deliberately dual-axis so you can see both the **stock** (remaining principal) and the **flow probability** (likelihood the loan has already disappeared) at the same time.
