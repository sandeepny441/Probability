# Early Pay Off (EPO) — Overview

## What is EPO?
An **Early Pay Off** occurs when a borrower pays off their loan (typically via refinance, sometimes home sale) within a short window after origination — usually **within the first 3–6 months**, though contracts may extend to 6, 9, or even 12 months.

## Why EPO Matters Economically
- **Investor / MSR Buyer**: Loses expected servicing cash flow, premium paid over par is not recouped, MSR premium destroyed.
- **Aggregator / Correlated Loan Buyer**: Paid a premium (e.g., 102-104) for the loan; if it pays off within the EPO window, most/all of that premium is lost.
- **EPO Recourse Clauses**: Purchase agreements typically require the originating lender to **return the premium** (or a scheduled portion) if a loan EPOs within the contract window.
- **Servicer**: Absorbs boarding cost ($30-$70/loan), earns only 1-3 months of servicing fee, and books an economic loss on MSR carried at premium.

## The Two Perspectives

### Investor / Loan Buyer Perspective
Focused on **avoiding paying premium for loans likely to prepay in the EPO window**. They screen loan tapes for red flags before bidding and price accordingly.

### Servicer / MSR Buyer Perspective
Focused on **not paying a high MSR multiple for loans likely to run off quickly**, and on **operational readiness** (boarding, retention outreach) for EPO-prone segments.

## Rule of Thumb
EPO risk is highest when borrower is **overpaying vs. market** (high SATO), **rate-aware / sophisticated**, **contacted by other lenders**, and originated by **broker channel with churn-heavy LO** at a **lender known for aggressive pricing without retention**.
